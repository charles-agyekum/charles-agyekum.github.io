The question

For an Adidas US sales manager: which retailers, regions, products and sales methods drive revenue and profit — to inform partner negotiations, stock allocation and channel strategy.

What I built

  1. 1 Audited first. A =UNIQUE() check on Retailer, Region, Sales Method and Product came back clean — no variants or stray spaces — documented in a consistency block so anyone opening the file sees the data was validated. Curated does not mean assumed.
  2. 2 One control set. Five pivot charts with three slicers (Retailer, Region, Product) and an Invoice Date timeline, all wired through Report Connections so every chart filters at once.
  3. 3 Tested the interactivity. Filtered to Region = West and confirmed every chart re-filtered correctly, including the Top-10 cities.

Findings & recommendation

Three retailers — West Gear, Foot Locker and Sports Direct — drive close to three-quarters (~72%) of revenue, so any partnership trim should come from the weaker tail, not the top.

Sales channels are balanced — roughly Online 38%, Outlet 34%, In-store 28% — so a shift online should be tested against profit per channel, not units alone. The product-by-retailer matrix shows Men’s Street Footwear over-indexing at Foot Locker and West Gear, pointing to where surplus stock should route.

Recommendation

Focus partner negotiations on the three retailers driving ~72% of revenue and trim from the weaker tail; test any channel shift against profit per channel, and route surplus Men’s Street Footwear to Foot Locker and West Gear where it over-indexes.

Why I can talk over it

In procurement I made weekly route-to-buyer decisions on surplus stock. The product-by-retailer matrix here is that same decision in another industry: where do I send excess so it actually sells.
